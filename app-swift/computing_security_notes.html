11.1

Defining Security & Privacy

Security is the practice of protecting the confidentiality, integrity and availability of data — applicable to all data types, protecting against unauthorised access, modification, or disruption. Think of it as a padlock on a door.

Privacy is the practice of protecting the confidentiality and control of personal data (name, DOB, address, email, IP, etc.) — including how it is used or shared. Think of it as curtains on a window.

Key Notes on Each Principle

Confidentiality breach — passwords/bank info stolen → identity theft, fraud, blackmail; trade secrets exposed → financial & legal consequences.

Integrity breach — data altered without permission → untrustworthy data; e.g. bank transactions redirected; medical records modified → incorrect treatment.

Availability breach — e.g. denial-of-service (DoS) attack floods a server so it can't respond to legitimate requests. Also, data corruption (errors/inconsistencies introduced) can render data unusable, affecting availability too.

Integrity → Availability link Corrupted data that other data depends on (e.g. a table header) can make the entire dataset unusable, not just the corrupted cells.
11.2

Threats

Human Actions

IntegrityPrivacy
Physical: dropping storage media, improper ejection.
Non-physical: overwriting shared files, incorrect data entry, sending personal data to wrong recipients.

Adware

SecurityPrivacy
Malware that installs itself silently and shows unwanted ads (pop-ups or injected into pages). May collect browsing data and promote phishing sites.

Spyware

ConfidentialityPrivacy
Hidden malware that secretly collects personal info and sends it to attackers — enabling further unauthorised access.

Cookies

Privacy
Small data files stored by browsers. Not inherently malicious, but ad networks can use them to track users across multiple sites.

Phishing

ConfidentialityPrivacy
Fake emails/messages/websites impersonating reputable companies to steal passwords and credit card details.

Pharming

ConfidentialityPrivacy
More severe than phishing — intercepts requests to a legitimate URL and silently redirects to a fake site. Requires malware on victim's device or control of a router/server.

Phishing vs Pharming Phishing relies on tricking the user into clicking a fake link. Pharming redirects automatically — the user types the correct URL but still lands on the fake site.
11.3

Defences

Anti-Malware Programs

Detects, blocks, and removes malware. Relies on a signature database that must be kept updated to detect new threats.

Firewalls

Hardware or software that monitors network packets and blocks/allows traffic based on configurable rules. Can block adware/spyware from phoning home.

Encryption

Encodes data so it appears random and meaningless without the secret key. Used to protect data in transit (e.g. website passwords).

PDPA (Singapore)

Legal protection. Organisations must: (1) seek consent, (2) disclose purpose, (3) retain data only as long as needed. Violations → heavy fines.

Good Computing Practices

Strong Passwords

Authentication verifies user identity. A strong password uses a mix of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols. Avoid birthdates/surnames. Use unique passwords per account; update every 90 days.

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Authentication factors: (1) something you know (password/PIN), (2) something you own (phone/security token), (3) something you are (biometrics). Two-factor authentication combines two categories — much harder to compromise.

Biometrics — fingerprint, face, voice, iris, DNA. More secure than passwords as characteristics are unique and hard to replicate.

Identify Scam Attempts

Signs of phishing emails/messages:

Requests for Personal Data

Legitimate companies/banks never ask for passwords or card details by email.

Errors

Spelling, grammar, or factual errors suggest the message isn't from a real source.

Generic Greetings

"Dear Customer" or "Dear User" — likely auto-generated. May get name right but other details wrong.

Suspicious Email Addresses

Free providers (Gmail/Yahoo), misspelled domain names, or random usernames.

Suspicious Links

Hover over links before clicking — check the actual destination URL.

Urgent / Too Good to Be True

Pressure tactics to act fast, or offers that seem unrealistic.

Other Good Practices

  • Update software regularly — patches fix bugs that malware exploits.
  • Manage cookies — configure browser to block/delete cookies from untrusted sites.
  • Regular backups — store on a separate device/online server; restores data if corruption or accidental overwrite occurs.
